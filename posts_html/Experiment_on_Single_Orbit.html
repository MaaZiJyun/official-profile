Experiment on Single Orbit Tasking Duration Minimisation

Zi Jyun Maa

May 2025

Introduction

This study explores whether a single-orbit satellite constellation can complete tasks fast enough for real-time applications. At first glance, a single orbit sounds simple and elegant—so we tested if it could actually work.

Our goal is to minimise the tasking duration: Ttask = tr − t0, the time from task creation to final data downlink.

For simplicity, each task includes only three phases:

(A) Tasking: receiving commands from the ground. (B) Observing: capturing images of the target. (C) Downlink: sending data back to Earth.

Two basic conditions must also be satisfied:

Condition 1 (GS Access): the satellite must be visible above a 10∘ elevation angle.

Condition 2 (RoI Observation): the target must fall inside 90% of the satellite’s field of view.

Experiment Setup

We conducted a full-factorial experiment using four variables:

  • Number of satellites: 1, 6, 12, 24

  • Number of ground stations: 1, 2, 4, 6

  • Ground station latitude band: low / mid / high

  • RoI latitude band: low / mid / high

This results in 4 × 4 × 3 × 3 = 144 scenario combinations.

Experiment Implementation

To avoid bias, each of the 10 experiment batches randomly generates new GS and RoI coordinates (within the selected latitude band). Each batch then tests all 144 parameter combinations.

Each simulation follows this workflow:

  • Generate orbits (500 km, 98° inclination) and evenly spaced satellites

  • Estimate orbit period T, number of shots, and step size dt = T/nshot

  • Simulate from first possible observation until successful downlink

  • Record the total task duration and parameters

Experiment Results

A total of 1,440 valid simulation rows were collected.

The satellites operate at 500 km altitude, 90° inclination, with a 30° FoV. The orbit period is 5668 s (1.57 h), and each satellite takes 180 shots per cycle. The maximum simulation duration is 3.149 hours with a 31.49 s step size.

The main source of failure occurs in the observing stage—satellites often cannot view the RoI in time. This is largely due to orbital inclination:

  • Polar satellites sweep north–south and rely on Earth’s rotation to cover longitude.

  • The Earth takes 24 hours to rotate once, so full coverage also takes 24 hours.

  • Our mission requires a response within only two orbital cycles—far too short for polar coverage.

Thus, many failures are not “errors” but physical limitations of polar orbits.

The task duration histogram shows two main clusters: a short group (0–1000 s), a larger group (6000–10,000 s), and a long tail up to 20,000 s.

Conclusion

  • High-latitude GS and RoI placements significantly shorten tasking duration.

  • Adding more satellites or GS helps, but latitude effects are even stronger.

  • A single polar orbit cannot guarantee real-time response.

  • A practical solution is to combine polar and low-inclination orbits.
